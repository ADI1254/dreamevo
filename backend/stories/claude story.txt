# INTO THE WORLD
### A 20-Minute Screenplay
*Based on "Into the Wild" - Told in Second Person POV*

---

## OPENING SEQUENCE

**FADE IN:**

You wake in darkness. Your mother's voice pierces through sleep—she's heard you calling for help. But you're not there. You're already gone.

---

## ACT ONE: THE DEPARTURE (Minutes 1-5)

### SCENE 1: THE DROP-OFF

A trucker drops you at the edge of wilderness. You leave everything on his dashboard—wallet, cards, the remnants of your old life.

"Keep it," you say.

He hands you rubber boots. "If you make it out alive, give me a call."

You shoulder your pack and walk into the wild. No phone, no pool, no pets, no cigarettes. Ultimate freedom. You are an extremist now, an aesthetic voyager whose home is the road.

### SCENE 2: THE BUS

You find it—an abandoned Fairbanks bus, rusted and beautiful. Your sanctuary.

"Hello? Is there anybody here?" Your voice echoes. "Guess not!"

You howl at the sky. This is it. The climactic battle to kill the false being within.

You carve into the wood: **Alexander Supertramp. May 1992.**

### FLASHBACK: GRADUATION DAY

You remember standing at your college graduation. Your parents smile, proud and oblivious. You see them as they were—young, about to marry, dumb and innocent.

You want to warn them: "Stop. Don't do it. You're going to do things you cannot imagine. You're going to do bad things to children. You're going to suffer."

But you don't. Instead, you say: "Do what you are going to do, and I will tell about it."

Your father offers you a new car. "That Datsun could blow up any day."

"I don't want a new car. I don't want anything. These things, things, things."

You measure yourself by a fiercely rigorous moral code. And you know—it's inevitable that you'll break away. When you do, it'll be with characteristic immoderation.

---

## ACT TWO: THE JOURNEY (Minutes 6-12)

### SCENE 3: RAINEY AND JAN

You meet them on the road—rubber tramps with a van and open hearts. Jan notices something about you immediately.

"Where are your mom and dad?" she asks.

"Living their lives somewhere."

"You look like a loved kid. Be fair."

You quote Thoreau: "Rather than love, than money, than faith, than fame, than fairness—give me truth."

Rainey shares his wisdom by the campfire. "It's important in life not necessarily to be strong, but to feel strong. To measure yourself at least once in the most ancient of human conditions—facing the blind, deaf stone alone with nothing to help you but your hands and your own head."

You test yourself against the ocean. The waves are brutal. Rainey holds Jan afterward, and they look at you with something like recognition—like you remind them of someone they once knew.

### SCENE 4: THE RAPIDS

You need a permit to paddle the river. Twelve years' wait.

"Twelve years? To paddle down a river?"

You don't wait. You launch anyway, screaming "I'm Supertramp!" as you ride the rapids without permission, without a helmet, without fear.

You paddle all the way to Mexico, navigating spillways and desert. A sandstorm takes your kayak. You walk back north, eating enough sand to send you back to the city.

### SCENE 5: WAYNE'S GRAIN ELEVATOR

You work the combines in South Dakota. Wayne becomes a friend—the kind of man who sees through your bravado to the hurt beneath.

"You can't be juggling blood and fire all the time," he tells you. "What 'people' are you talking about anyway?"

"Parents. Hypocrites. Politicians. Pricks."

"Alex, you're a hell of a young guy. But I promise you—you can't be doing this forever."

When the FBI raids Wayne's operation, he tells you: "Come back and work for me anytime. But remember—no Alaska till spring. South, kid. Head south."

### FLASHBACK: THE FAMILY SECRET

Your sister Carine narrates what you discovered that changed everything:

You found out your father was already married when he met your mother. You and Carine—bastard children. The revelation murdered every day's truth. Your entire childhood became fiction.

Daily bouts of rage. Violence you were forced to witness. Your parents casting you as both judges and accused.

You never told them you knew. You made Carine promise silence.

That's when you decided. That's when the road became necessary.

### SCENE 6: RON FRANZ

An old leatherworker finds you camping in the desert. Eighty years old, lonely, still grieving his wife and son killed by a drunk driver decades ago.

"Don't you think you ought to be getting an education? Making something of this life?"

"I think careers are a 20th century invention. I don't want one. I'm living like this by choice."

You take him climbing. He's terrified but does it anyway—for you. At the summit, he gasps, alive for the first time in years.

"I'm gonna miss you when you go," he says.

"The joy of life doesn't come principally from human relationships. God's placed it all around us."

Ron looks at you with tears in his eyes. "When you forgive, you love. And when you love, God's light shines on you."

Before you leave, he asks: "What do you say you let me adopt you? I can be your grandfather."

You promise to talk about it when you get back from Alaska.

---

## ACT THREE: ALASKA & THE RECKONING (Minutes 13-20)

### SCENE 7: HUNTING

You shoot your first big game—a moose. Triumph surges through you. You can survive. You can do this.

But you make a mistake. You don't preserve the meat fast enough. Flies arrive. Maggots. The entire kill spoils.

You scream at the sky: "Where are the fucking animals now? I'm hungry! I'm fucking hungry!"

### SCENE 8: THE PLANT

You forage carefully, consulting your guidebook. You identify each plant by its right name.

Hedysarum alpinum. Wild potato root. Safe.

You eat.

Days later, you're violently ill. Vomiting, weak. Something was wrong. The plant—it wasn't what you thought. Or maybe it was, but in the wrong season, with the wrong toxins.

You realize: you're trapped. The river's too high to cross. You're too weak to walk out.

### SCENE 9: THE REALIZATION

You write in your journal, your handwriting growing shaky:

*"Happiness only real when shared."*

You think of Ron. Of Rainey and Jan. Of Wayne. Of Tracy at the Slabs. Of your parents, imperfect and flawed but *human*.

You were wrong. Not about society's sickness—but about the cure. The cure wasn't isolation. It was connection. It was forgiveness.

### SCENE 10: THE END

Your body fails. You crawl into your sleeping bag in the bus. You write one final entry:

*"I have had a happy life and thank the Lord. Goodbye and may God bless all."*

You close your eyes. Images flood your mind:

Your mother's face. Your father's hand on your shoulder. Carine's laughter. The ocean with Rainey. Ron at the mountain summit. Jan making you a hat. Wayne teaching you to combine.

You see what could have been: returning home, older and wiser, ready to forgive. Ready to be forgiven. Ready to build something real from the ashes of your family's mistakes.

But it's too late now.

You smile. You've measured yourself against the ancient conditions. You've felt strong. You've been free.

Your last thought: *"What if I were smiling and running into your arms? Would you see then what I see now?"*

---

## CLOSING

**CARINE (V.O.):**

You wrote your story, Chris. And it had to be you who told it. Two years you walked the earth. You found what you were looking for—and you found what you didn't know you needed until it was too late to use it.

You died alone in that bus. But your story—it makes us remember. It makes us forgive. It makes us love.

Because that's what you learned, isn't it?

That happiness is only real when shared.

---

**FADE TO BLACK.**

**THE END**

---

## RUNTIME: Approximately 20 minutes
## TONE: Contemplative, raw, spiritually searching
## POV: Second person present tense throughout
## KEY THEMES: Freedom vs. connection, idealism vs. reality, forgiveness, the cost of truth
